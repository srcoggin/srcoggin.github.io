NFL commissioner Roger Goodell said Monday the league will take a closer look at the Rooney Rule and “all that it encompasses,” a public acknowledgment that the NFL’s flagship diversity-hiring initiative faces renewed scrutiny after a coaching cycle that produced minimal change at the top of coaching staffs. The comments came after an offseason in which only one of the league’s 10 head-coaching vacancies was filled by a minority candidate, a result that again raised questions about whether current hiring processes are delivering on the rule’s original intent.

Goodell’s remarks underscored the stakes for a league that remains heavily reliant on Black and minority players but continues to see far less diversity in head coaching and front-office roles. The commissioner described the outcome as evidence there is “more work to do,” according to ESPN, signaling that the NFL plans to reassess not just the rule’s interview requirements but the broader system of how teams identify, evaluate and ultimately select candidates for the most influential jobs in a franchise.

The Rooney Rule, adopted in 2003, was designed to expand opportunities by requiring teams to interview minority candidates for head coaching openings and certain senior football operations roles. Over time, the league has adjusted the policy and added related measures aimed at strengthening the pipeline, but Goodell’s comments highlighted an ongoing tension: Compliance with the rule’s procedures has not consistently translated into diverse hires. With nine of 10 head-coaching positions going to non-minority candidates this cycle, the league is again confronting a gap between process and outcome that fans, advocacy groups and many within the sport have criticized for years.

The issue matters beyond optics. Head coaches set competitive direction, build staffs, shape player development and often drive roster-building philosophy in partnership with general managers. For a league built on parity and constant coaching turnover, who gets considered — and who gets hired — can influence everything from on-field innovation to locker-room culture. It also affects aspiring coaches and executives throughout the league, where advancement is frequently tied to networking, mentorship and access to decision-makers.

Goodell did not specify what changes could come next, but his emphasis on reviewing “all that it encompasses” points toward a broader examination of how teams conduct searches, how candidates are sourced, and how interview processes are structured.
